Understanding qSOFA Scores

Jan 04, 2026 By Clinical Staff 5 min read

The Quick Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (qSOFA) score is a bedside prompt that may identify patients with suspected infection who are at greater risk for a poor outcome outside the intensive care unit (ICU). It uses three simple criteria, assigning one point for low blood pressure (systolic < 100 mmHg), high respiratory rate (> 22 breaths/min), or altered mentation (GCS < 15).

Despite debates over its sensitivity versus SIRS, qSOFA remains a crucial tool for rapid triage due to its simplicity. It requires no laboratory tests, making it immediately applicable in emergency departments and general wards worldwide.

The 3 Criteria (HAT)

  • Hypotension: Systolic blood pressure ≤ 100 mmHg.
  • Altered Mental Status: Any score less than 15 on the Glasgow Coma Scale, or simply being confused/disoriented.
  • Tachypnea: Respiratory rate ≥ 22 breaths per minute.

A qSOFA score of ≥ 2 suggests a greater risk of a poor outcome. These patients should be assessed for evidence of organ dysfunction and monitored closely.

Limitations and Context

It is important to note that qSOFA is a prognostic tool, not a diagnostic one. A low qSOFA score does not rule out sepsis, but a high score is a strong red flag for likely deterioration.
