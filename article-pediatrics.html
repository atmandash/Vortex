Sepsis in Pediatrics: Early Signs

Feb 12, 2026 By Dr. Emily Chen 4 min read

Diagnosing sepsis in children is notoriously difficult. Unlike adults, children often maintain their blood pressure until they are in late-stage shock ("compensated shock"), making conventional vitals misleading.

Early recognition is key. Parents are often the first to notice that "something isn't right." This guide outlines specific red flags for pediatric sepsis that go beyond simple fever.

Key Red Flags in Children

  • Mottled Skin: Patchy, discolored skin, or unusually cold hands and feet despite a fever.
  • Lethargy: The child is unusually difficult to wake or is not interacting. "Floppy" appearance in infants.
  • Fast Breathing: Tachypnea without a cough or runny nose can be a sign of metabolic acidosis.
  • No Urine Output: Dry diapers for more than 12 hours.

"If a parent says their child is 'not acting right' or this is the sickest they've ever seen them, we take that extremely seriously," says Dr. Emily Chen, pediatric intensivist.

Treatment Differences

Fluid resuscitation protocols ("pushes") are strictly weight-based (typically 20ml/kg). Over-hydration can be dangerous, so careful monitoring is needed. Antibiotics should be administered within the first hour of suspected sepsis.
